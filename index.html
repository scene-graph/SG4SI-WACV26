Scene graphs provide a structured and interpretable representation of objects, attributes, and relationships in 2D, 3D, and even 4D scenes, serving as a vital bridge between raw visual data and high-level reasoning, which is critical for tasks such as visual reasoning, navigation, and embodied AI. With the rapid rise of multimodal foundation models, integrating scene graphs has become a timely and essential task, offering controllability, explainability, and stronger generalization across different domains and modalities.

This workshop will highlight the latest advances in scene graph generation, representation learning, and their applications in vision–language reasoning, multimodal generation, and robotics. We aim to establish new benchmarks, foster interdisciplinary collaboration, and chart future directions toward the development of structured multimodal intelligence. By uniting researchers from computer vision, NLP, and robotics, the workshop will stimulate impactful discussions and accelerate progress toward trustworthy, general-purpose AI systems.

Submission Guidelines

Paper Types

We welcome submissions of long papers (up to 8 pages excluding references), short papers (up to 4 pages plus references), and demo papers (up to 4 pages plus references).

Call for Presentations: We accept presentations of papers that have already been accepted by WACV or other proceedings. You may also submit your paper together with proof of acceptance (e.g., acceptance letter or reviews).

Format Requirements

All papers must be written in English, follow the double-blind review process, and conform to the WACV formatting guidelines. Templates are available on the WACV website.

Submission System

Papers should be submitted via the OpenReview system. Authors will indicate paper type and proceedings inclusion preference.

Publication

Authors can choose whether their submission appears in workshop proceedings. We particularly encourage long papers to be included.

Charu Sharma

Assistant Professor

International Institute of Information Technology Hyderabad (IIIT-H)

Talk Title

3D Scene Understanding Through Robust Scene Graph Generation

Abstract

Scene understanding and reasoning have been a fundamental problem in 3D computer vision, requiring models to identify objects, their properties, and spatial or comparative relationships among the objects. Existing approaches enable this by creating scene graphs using multiple inputs such as 2D images, depth maps, object labels, and annotated relationships from a specific reference view. However, these methods often struggle with generalization and produce inaccurate spatial relationships like "left/right", which become inconsistent across different viewpoints. To address these limitations, we work on advancing scene graph generation for 3D scene Reasoning. This talk will highlight recent advances in 3D SGG, discuss challenges like multi-view consistency and ambiguity, and explore emerging research directions that aim to bring structured intelligence to embodied AI systems.

Bio

Charu Sharma is an assistant professor at the Machine Learning Lab at the International Institute of Information Technology Hyderabad (IIIT-H), with publications at AAAI, ICML, NeurIPS, ECCV, WACV, and other venues. Her research interests include geometric machine learning and deep learning—specifically 3D point clouds, 3D scene understanding, 3D+LLM, and graph/temporal graph/knowledge graph representation learning. She has been serving as a reviewer at top-tier conferences such as ICML, NeurIPS, CVPR, AAAI, ICLR, etc.

Tuan Dang

Assistant Professor

University of Arkansas

Talk Title

Scene Graphs as Spatial-Temporal Reasoning Substrates for Zero-Shot Visual Navigation

Abstract

Robust visual navigation in unseen environments requires structured representations that go beyond geometry or end-to-end policy learning. Scene graphs provide an object-centric abstraction for encoding visual, semantic, and spatial information. Yet most navigation systems rely on static graphs that fail under viewpoint changes and long-horizon exploration. This talk examines scene graphs as spatial-temporal reasoning substrates for zero-shot visual navigation, a training-free navigation framework, as a case study. We formalize scene graphs as attributed relational graphs grounded in RGB-D observations and introduce temporal scene graph memory, which models object permanence through cross-temporal correspondences between evolving graphs without learned parameters. This enables consistent instance tracking and reframes from instance-image navigation as a graph-matching problem rather than a policy-learning problem. We further discuss how scene-graph-based reasoning integrates with topological trajectory analysis to reduce redundant exploration and improve navigation efficiency in unseen environments.

Bio

Tuan Dang is a tenure-track Assistant Professor of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science at the University of Arkansas. He earned his Ph.D. in Computer Science and Engineering from the University of Texas at Arlington, USA, in 2025, under the supervision of [Dr. Manfred Huber](https://www.uta.edu/academics/faculty/profile?user=huber). Prior to that, he obtained a master's degree in electrical and computer engineering from Sungkyunkwan University in South Korea in 2013. During that time, he worked as a project manager, developing real-time robot controllers using EtherCAT technology for various industrial robots, which have been adopted by numerous manufacturers. His research interests focus on robot learning, particularly the representation of real-time observations, bio-inspired navigation, and manipulation.

Manolis Savva

Associate Professor

Simon Fraser University

Talk Title

Digital Twins for Embodied AI: Advancing the Frontiers of Realism & Interaction

Abstract

3D digital twins and simulators are increasingly being used to develop and evaluate embodied AI agents that perceive and act in realistic environments. Much of the prior work in this space has treated simulators as "black boxes" within which learning algorithms are to be deployed. However, the system characteristics of the simulation platforms themselves and the datasets that are used with these platforms both greatly impact the feasibility and the outcomes of experiments involving simulation. In this talk, I will describe recent projects that outline emerging challenges and opportunities in the development of 3D simulation for embodied AI, in particular focusing on controllable generation of articulated objects and interactive 3D scenes for creating digital twins of interior spaces..

Bio

Manolis Savva is an Associate Professor at Simon Fraser University, and a Canada Research Chair in Computer Graphics.  His research focuses on analysis, organization and generation of 3D content.  Prior to his current position he was a visiting researcher at Facebook AI Research, and a postdoctoral researcher at Princeton University.  He received his Ph.D. from Stanford University under the supervision of Pat Hanrahan.  His work has been recognized through several awards including an ACM UIST notable paper award (ReVision), an ICCV best paper nomination (Habitat), two SGP dataset awards (ShapeNet, SGP 2018; ScanNet, SGP 2020), the 2022 Graphics Interface early career researcher award, and an ICLR 2023 outstanding paper award (Emergence of Maps).
